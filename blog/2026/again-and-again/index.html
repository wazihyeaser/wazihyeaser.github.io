Again and Again and Again.....

Created on July 23, 2026

2026   ·   habits   productivity   focus   ·   book review

The title of this post is a joke I stole from my own to-do list. For years the same three or four items just kept reappearing. Learn this. Finish that. Focus. I used to read the repetition as failure. Three books — James Clear’s Atomic Habits, Charles Duhigg’s The Power of Habit, and Cal Newport’s Deep Work — slowly convinced me it was the opposite. The repetition wasn’t the problem. It was the entire mechanism.

Start with the machinery. Duhigg’s central idea is that a habit is a loop with three parts: a cue that triggers it, a routine you run, and a reward that makes the loop worth repeating. Do it often enough and the brain stops deliberating — it hands the whole sequence to autopilot to save energy. Clear keeps the loop but sharpens it, slipping a fourth piece into the middle: craving. The cue doesn’t just start the routine; it starts a wanting, and the wanting is the engine. It is the same diagram drawn at higher resolution, and the extra piece matters, because craving is where a habit is actually won or lost.

What neither of them lets you keep is the flattering story we tell about success — that it comes from motivation, or willpower, or the occasional heroic burst of effort. Both books are quietly ruthless about this. Willpower runs out. Motivation is a mood. What’s left, when those fail, is whatever your habits do on their own. Clear’s version is that tiny actions, one per cent better, compound into results that look sudden only because nobody was watching the arithmetic. Duhigg’s is the “keystone habit” — the single routine that, once changed, quietly rewrites a dozen others. Different metaphors, same claim: success is mostly the residue of what you repeat without thinking.

But repetition alone isn’t the whole story, and this is where the third book earns its place. Newport’s Deep Work is not about habit at all, on its surface. It is about attention — his argument that the ability to focus without distraction on something cognitively demanding is both increasingly rare and increasingly valuable, and that most of what fills a workday is shallow busywork that feels productive and produces little. His formula stuck with me: high-quality work equals time spent multiplied by the intensity of your focus. Not hours. Hours times depth.

The thing nobody told me is that these are the same idea. Newport insists that deep focus is not a gift you either have or lack — it is a skill you train, and you train it exactly the way you would build any habit: with rituals, fixed times, a set place, and a stubborn refusal to reach for your phone the moment the work turns boring. His “rituals” are just Duhigg’s cues and routines wearing a lab coat. Deep work is a habit. And habits, in turn, are what make deep work possible: every trivial decision you have automated is a scrap of attention you didn’t spend, saved up for the one hour that matters.

So the chain, once I saw it, was hard to unsee. Habits automate the small things so your finite attention isn’t bled away on them. That freed attention is what deep work spends, all at once, on the work that is genuinely hard. Deep work is where the valuable output comes from. And the output, repeated, is what compounds into whatever we end up calling success. Habit is the foundation, focus is the peak, and neither does much without the other. A person with good habits and no depth stays busy and shallow; a person capable of depth but with no habits burns out chasing it on willpower alone.

What all three books share, underneath the productivity-shelf packaging, is a genuinely unglamorous psychology. You are not the person you intend to be; you are the person you repeat. Your attention is neither infinite nor free. And the way you get good at anything worth being good at is not a burst — it is a loop, run deliberately, protected fiercely, until the doing of it no longer requires a decision.

Which is a long way of saying the thing my to-do list was trying to tell me all along. The items that keep coming back aren’t proof that you’re failing. They’re just the ones that count. So you do them again. And again. And again.
