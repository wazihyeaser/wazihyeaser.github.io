For a while I went on a strange reading run — five books that, on paper, have almost nothing to do with each other. Two are about the wiring of the human mind. One is about how to change it. One is about sex and evolution. One is about why some societies ended up conquering others. I did not plan it as a course in anything. But somewhere in the middle I noticed they were all quietly telling the same uncomfortable story.

The story is this: the answer that feels obvious is usually the wrong one, and thinking well is mostly the work of distrusting it.

Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* and Dobelli's *The Art of Thinking Clearly* make the case most directly. Both argue that the mind runs, by default, on fast and automatic shortcuts — and that those shortcuts fail in patterned, predictable ways. What struck me was that they say almost the same thing at different altitudes. Kahneman gives you the machinery: two systems, one impulsive and one effortful, with the lazy one usually winning. Dobelli skips the theory and hands you the field guide — ninety-nine named traps, from survivorship bias to the sunk-cost fallacy, each with a one-line warning. One explains why you err; the other helps you catch yourself doing it. You need both, which is why it never felt like reading the same book twice.

Adam Grant's *Think Again* adds the move the other two leave implicit. Knowing your biases, it turns out, does almost nothing to stop them. The rarer skill is rethinking — holding your own conclusions loosely, treating beliefs as hypotheses rather than possessions, learning to feel curiosity instead of threat when you turn out to be wrong. Grant's ideal is the scientist: someone who prefers doubt to certainty on purpose. Where Kahneman and Dobelli catalogue the errors, Grant says the errors will keep coming, and the only defence is a posture, not a checklist.

Then the run took a turn I didn't expect. Matt Ridley's *The Red Queen* is about evolution — why sex exists, how the endless arms race with parasites and rivals shaped us. But it reframed the whole bias conversation for me. Ridley's point is that human nature is not designed; it is the residue of that race, each generation running flat out just to stay in place. And that means the mind's "flaws" are not really flaws. A brain that jumps to patterns, fears loss more than it values gain, and trusts its own tribe was not malfunctioning on the savanna — it was surviving. This is where two versions of one fact both turn out to be true. Loss aversion is a bug in a spreadsheet and a feature on the plains; the mind is not broken, it is superbly built for a world that has stopped existing. Whether it counts as an error depends entirely on which environment you grade it against.

Jared Diamond's *Guns, Germs, and Steel* is the same lesson at the scale of continents. The intuitive explanation for why some peoples dominated others is the flattering one — that they were cleverer, or harder, or simply better. Diamond spends the whole book dismantling exactly that story and replacing it with something duller and truer: geography. Wheat and cattle, an east–west axis, the accident of which animals could be domesticated and which diseases you had already survived. It is the most self-effacing kind of thinking there is — refusing the narrative that makes you the hero. And tellingly, his own critics accuse him of going too far, of letting geography erase human choices. They may be right too. Cause at one scale doesn't cancel cause at another; the map and the decisions can both matter.

That is the thread. A psychologist, a writer, an organisational scientist, an evolutionary biologist, and a geographer, none of them talking to each other, all arriving in the same place. The comfortable answer flatters you. The true one usually costs you something — your certainty, your specialness, your story. "Thinking right" turned out not to be a set of correct opinions I could collect. It was closer to a stance: distrust the easy explanation, look underneath it for the structural one, and then, having found it, hold even that loosely enough to put back down.

Right thinking, it seems, is mostly the willingness to think again.
